Solid Phase Extraction (SPE)

SPE is the sample preparation technique you'll use to prepare your beverage sample before GC-MS analysis. The fundamental problem is this: your beverages are water-based, and water cannot be injected into a GC. It would destroy the column's stationary phase and ruin the separation. SPE solves this by transferring caffeine out of the aqueous matrix and into an organic solvent (acetonitrile) that is GC-compatible. Along the way, SPE also removes interfering matrix components and concentrates your analyte, but getting rid of the water is the essential first step. Understanding why each step matters will help you troubleshoot problems in the lab.

Work through the numbered sections below in order, then complete the concept checks at the bottom.

Solid Phase Extraction is a sample preparation technique that uses a solid adsorbent (packed in a small cartridge) to selectively isolate target analytes from a complex sample matrix. Think of it as a selective filter: your beverage contains sugars, proteins, pigments, and hundreds of other compounds, but you only care about caffeine.

The critical reason we need SPE in this experiment is solvent exchange: your samples are aqueous (water-based), but GC requires the analyte to be dissolved in a volatile organic solvent. Water injected into a GC would damage the column and make separation impossible. SPE lets us trap caffeine from the water, wash away the aqueous matrix, and then elute caffeine into acetonitrile — a solvent the GC can handle. Beyond this essential solvent exchange, SPE also (1) removes interfering matrix components that would obscure your GC-MS signal, (2) concentrates your analyte so it's above the detection limit, and (3) improves reproducibility compared to liquid-liquid extraction.

In this experiment, you'll use a CHROMABOND C18 ec cartridge, a reversed-phase adsorbent. The C18 (octadecyl) chains are nonpolar, so they retain nonpolar and moderately polar molecules like caffeine from an aqueous sample.

There are two general approaches in SPE, depending on the chemistry of your analyte and matrix:

Strategy 1: Retain the analyte: The analyte adsorbs onto the sorbent while interfering matrix components flow through. After a wash step, the analyte is eluted with a stronger solvent. This is what you'll do with caffeine on C18.

Strategy 2: Retain the interferences: The analyte flows through the cartridge while unwanted matrix components stick to the sorbent. The analyte is collected directly in the flow-through. This approach is used when matrix components are more strongly retained than the analyte.

Choosing between these strategies depends on the polarity of the analyte relative to the matrix and the sorbent chemistry. For reversed-phase sorbents like C18, nonpolar analytes in aqueous matrices are well-retained, making Strategy 1 ideal for caffeine in water-based beverages.

Your CHROMABOND C18 ec cartridge (1 mL / 100 mg) consists of a small polypropylene syringe barrel packed with 100 mg of silica-based sorbent, held in place by two polyethylene frits (filter elements) at the top and bottom.

The sorbent: Silica particles are chemically bonded with C18 (18-carbon) alkyl chains. These long hydrocarbon chains create a nonpolar surface. When an aqueous sample passes through, nonpolar and moderately polar molecules (like caffeine) partition out of the water and onto the C18 surface via hydrophobic interactions (van der Waals forces).

The "ec" designation stands for "endcapped," meaning residual silanol groups on the silica surface have been chemically capped. This reduces unwanted secondary interactions (e.g., with basic compounds) and improves reproducibility.

The cartridge connects to the vacuum manifold via a Luer fitting, a standardized taper that ensures a leak-free seal.

A vacuum manifold allows you to process multiple SPE cartridges simultaneously. It consists of a rectangular glass chamber with a polypropylene lid that holds the cartridges on top. Test tubes or collection vessels sit inside the chamber below the cartridges.

How it works: A vacuum pump reduces the pressure inside the chamber. This pressure differential draws liquid through the cartridge from top to bottom. The control valve on the side lets you adjust the vacuum level, and individual stopcocks on the lid allow independent flow control for each cartridge position.

Why vacuum? Gravity alone is often too slow and inconsistent. The vacuum provides controlled, reproducible flow rates. You'll aim for about 1–2 drops per second. Consistent flow is critical because too fast a flow can reduce the contact time between analyte and sorbent, lowering recovery.

Key practical point: Always close the valve first, add your solvent/sample to the cartridge, then carefully open the valve to start flow. This prevents sudden surges that could damage the sorbent bed or cause channeling.

Step 1 of 8

Beverage Preparation

Prepare your caffeinated beverage if you haven't already. For coffee or tea, brew it in a beaker and cool to room temperature in a water bath. For carbonated drinks, shake vigorously to remove dissolved CO₂.

Once cooled and degassed, dilute the beverage 1:10 with Milli-Q water: pipette 4.5 mL of water into a PE test tube, add 0.5 mL of beverage, and homogenize.

The dilution reduces viscosity and the concentration of matrix components that could clog the cartridge or compete for sorbent sites during extraction.

Step 2 of 8

Conditioning

Place an SPE cartridge on the vacuum manifold and position a test tube underneath. Add ~1 mL of acetonitrile (ACN) to the dry cartridge. Wait until no air bubbles are visible, then apply vacuum gently (1–2 drops/sec).

This wets the C18 chains and activates the sorbent surface. Think of it like priming a sponge: a dry sponge repels water, but once wetted, it absorbs readily.

Caution: Flow ACN until the liquid level reaches the top of the sorbent bed, but don't let it run dry! A dry sorbent must be re-conditioned from scratch.
Step 3 of 8

Equilibration

Rinse the cartridge with ~1 mL of water (the same solvent as your sample matrix).

This replaces the organic solvent with an aqueous environment, matching the conditions your sample will encounter. It ensures that when the aqueous sample arrives, the sorbent is ready to retain nonpolar analytes from water, not still saturated with organic solvent, which would reduce retention.

Caution: Do not let the sorbent run dry between conditioning and equilibration, or between equilibration and sample loading.
Step 4 of 8

Sample Loading

Add 300 µL of the 1:10 diluted beverage to the cartridge. Apply vacuum to draw it through at 1–2 drops/sec.

As the aqueous sample passes over the C18 sorbent, caffeine (and other hydrophobic compounds) partitions out of the water phase and onto the nonpolar C18 surface. Polar matrix components (sugars, salts, small organic acids) pass through largely unretained.

Step 5 of 8

Washing

Wash with ~1 mL of water. Apply vacuum until all liquid has passed through.

This removes any remaining polar interferences that are loosely associated with the sorbent or trapped in the interstitial spaces. Caffeine, being more strongly retained on C18, stays put. The wash solvent must be weak enough not to elute the analyte.

Remove the collection vessel and discard the wash (non-halogenated solvent waste).

Step 6 of 8

Elution

Weigh a clean test tube, then place it below the cartridge. Elute with 1 mL of acetonitrile by applying vacuum. Once no further eluate can be collected, re-weigh the tube.

Acetonitrile disrupts the hydrophobic interactions between caffeine and the C18 surface. The caffeine partitions back into the liquid phase and is collected in your tube.

The mass difference gives the eluate volume gravimetrically (density of ACN: 0.782 g/mL at 20 °C). This is more accurate than relying on the nominal 1 mL, since some liquid is always retained in the sorbent bed.

Step 7 of 8

Drying & GC Vial Transfer

Add anhydrous MgSO₄ in small portions (start with one spatula tip) to the eluate. Shake the tube; if the powder clumps at the bottom, add more until it floats freely.

MgSO₄ binds residual water that co-eluted with the acetonitrile. This is a critical final safeguard: as discussed earlier, water is incompatible with GC — even small amounts would damage the column's stationary phase and degrade separation quality. The drying step ensures your extract is truly water-free before injection.

Centrifuge for 60 s at 5000 rpm (add a counterweight!). Carefully transfer the clear supernatant into a GC vial with insert using a Pasteur pipette, ensuring no air bubbles are trapped. The sample is now ready for GC-MS.

Step 8 of 8

Standard Addition (Spiking)

After measuring your unspiked extract, prepare a spiked sample. The goal is to approximately double the caffeine concentration in the eluate to assess extraction recovery.

Calculate the needed spike volume (50–700 µL) from the 1 mg/mL caffeine standard. Dilute it if needed. Condition a fresh SPE cartridge and load 300 µL of the 1:10 diluted sample together with the calculated spike volume.

Perform the same SPE procedure (wash → elute → dry → transfer) and measure the spiked extract by GC-MS. Comparing it to the unspiked result gives you the percent recovery.

GC-MS Fundamentals

Gas chromatography coupled with mass spectrometry is the analytical technique you'll use to detect and quantify caffeine. The GC separates the mixture into individual compounds over time; the MS further separates ions by mass-to-charge ratio and detects them. Identification of compounds is then performed during data analysis by matching mass spectra against reference libraries.

Work through the numbered sections below in order, then complete the concept checks at the bottom.

Gas chromatography (GC) separates volatile compounds in a mixture. The principle is simple: a liquid sample is injected into a hot inlet where it vaporizes. A carrier gas (helium in your experiment) sweeps the vapor into a long, thin column coated on the inside with a chemical film (the stationary phase).

As the vaporized compounds travel through the column, they interact with the stationary phase. Compounds with stronger affinity for the stationary phase move more slowly; compounds with weaker affinity move faster. This differential migration separates the mixture into individual components that exit the column at different retention times).

Your column: HP-5MS Ultra Inert, 30 m × 0.25 mm ID × 0.25 µm film, a (5%-phenyl)-methylpolysiloxane stationary phase. This is a general-purpose, low-polarity column suitable for a wide range of analytes including caffeine.

Your method parameters: Injection in split mode (50:1), 1 µL injection volume, helium carrier gas at 2.5 mL/min, oven temperature programmed from 120 °C (1 min hold) → 30 °C/min → 270 °C (3 min hold). Total run time: 9 minutes.

The inlet (injector): Set to 250 °C, hot enough to instantly vaporize your sample. The "split" mode (ratio 50:1) means only 1/50th of the vaporized sample enters the column; the rest is vented. This prevents column overload for concentrated samples and sharpens peaks.

Temperature programming: Rather than holding the oven at a constant temperature, you ramp it upward. This is crucial because different compounds have different boiling points. Starting low allows volatile compounds to separate cleanly; ramping up pushes higher-boiling compounds off the column in a reasonable time. Without a temperature ramp, high-boiling compounds would emerge as extremely broad, undetectable peaks.

Carrier gas flow: Helium is inert and has good thermal conductivity. The constant flow rate of 2.5 mL/min ensures consistent compound transport through the column.

Why do we need GC separation before MS? A mass spectrometer would be overwhelmed by an unseparated mixture. Ions from different compounds would overlap, making identification and quantification impossible. GC delivers compounds one at a time (ideally) to the MS.

After the GC column, compounds enter the mass selective detector (MSD), a single-quadrupole mass spectrometer. The key steps are ionization, mass filtering, and detection.

Ionization (EI at 70 eV): Compounds are bombarded by a beam of high-energy electrons (70 eV) from a heated filament. This is called Electron Ionization (EI)). The electron impact knocks an electron out of the analyte molecule, creating a positively charged radical cation (the molecular ion, M⁺·). The excess energy also causes reproducible fragmentation, breaking the molecule into characteristic fragment ions. This fragmentation pattern is like a molecular fingerprint.

The vacuum system: The analyzer operates under high vacuum (maintained by a foreline + turbo/diffusion pump). Without high vacuum, ions would collide with air molecules and never reach the detector. The mean free path of ions must be long enough for them to travel the length of the quadrupole.

The quadrupole mass filter: Four parallel metal rods with combined DC and RF (radio frequency) voltages. At any given voltage setting, only ions of one specific m/z (mass-to-charge ratio) have a stable trajectory through the rods and reach the detector. All other ions collide with the rods and are lost. By rapidly scanning through different voltages, the quadrupole sequentially allows different m/z values to pass.

The detector (HED + electron multiplier): Ions exiting the quadrupole strike a high-energy conversion dynode, producing secondary electrons that are amplified by an electron multiplier. The resulting electrical signal is proportional to the number of ions, and thus to the amount of analyte.

Scan mode: The quadrupole sweeps across a wide mass range (in your case, m/z 45–200), recording the full mass spectrum at each time point. This is excellent for identification: you get the complete fragmentation pattern and can match it against library databases (like NIST). The downside: sensitivity is lower because the quadrupole spends only a fraction of its time on any single ion.

SIM (Selected Ion Monitoring) mode: Instead of scanning the full range, the quadrupole sits on just a few pre-selected m/z values and monitors them continuously. This dramatically increases sensitivity (10–100× improvement) because the detector spends all its time collecting signal for those specific ions.

Your method uses Scan/SIM simultaneously. The SIM ions for caffeine are:

• m/z 194, the molecular ion [M]⁺· (quantifier ion, used for quantification)
• m/z 109, a qualifier ion (confirms identity)
• m/z 67, a second qualifier ion

The quantifier ion (194) provides the peak area used for your calibration curve. The qualifier ions (109, 67) are used as a cross-check: if the ratios between the qualifier and quantifier ions in your sample match the ratios in your standard, you can be confident you're actually measuring caffeine and not a co-eluting interferent.

Quantification Strategies

Getting a peak on the GC-MS is only half the battle. Turning that signal into a reliable concentration requires careful calibration, and an awareness of what can go wrong.

Work through the numbered sections below in order, then complete the concept checks at the bottom.

The simplest way to turn a peak area into a concentration is a one-point calibration. You measure a single standard of known concentration and assume that the detector response is directly proportional to concentration, i.e. the calibration line passes through the origin.

cstandard Astandard
× Asample

The ratio cstandard / Astandard is the response factor: how much concentration each unit of peak area represents.

In this experiment, you measure a caffeine standard (c = 0.03 mg/mL) in triplicate at the start of Day 1, before the full calibration curve is ready. After your first sample eluate is measured, you use the one-point equation above to make a rough estimate of the caffeine concentration in your eluate. That estimate is all you need to calculate how much spike to add for the standard addition (target: approximately double the caffeine content).

Why not use this for final quantification? One-point calibration assumes that the calibration line passes through zero (b = 0). In practice, a non-zero y-intercept is common, caused by baseline offsets, chemical noise, or small interferences. Ignoring this intercept introduces systematic error, especially at low concentrations where b can be a significant fraction of the signal. A multi-point calibration curve fits both slope and intercept, and lets you evaluate linearity, identify the working range, and apply weighting if needed [6].

One-point calibration is therefore a useful screening tool for rapid estimates, good enough for calculating your spike volume, but not a substitute for a proper external calibration curve when reporting final results.

In external calibration, you prepare a series of standards with known concentrations of the analyte (caffeine), measure them on the GC-MS, and plot peak area (signal response) versus concentration. The resulting curve is your calibration function.

To determine the concentration in your unknown sample, you measure its peak area and read back the corresponding concentration from the calibration curve. This is the approach you'll use for final quantification in this experiment.

Why "external"? Because the standards are measured in separate injections from your sample, as opposed to internal calibration, where a reference compound is added directly to each sample.

Compared to the one-point estimate, a multi-point curve fits both slope and intercept (y = mx + b) and lets you evaluate linearity across the working range. A linear regression is preferred. If the response is non-linear at high concentrations, a quadratic fit (second-order polynomial) can be used instead. In your experiment report, you'll present the calibration curve including the regression function and R² value.

Three features define the quality of a calibration curve:

(1) Number of calibration points: More points = better definition of the regression line. For most bioanalytical work where ±15% accuracy is acceptable, five or six calibrators with two replicates each are a good starting point. You can get away with fewer for a narrow range, but below three points you can't meaningfully define a line.

(2) Placement of points: Ideally, points are spread across the entire expected concentration range. If your sample concentration is very low, adding extra calibrators near the lower limit of quantification (LLOQ) improves accuracy in that critical region.

(3) Replicates: Repeated measurements at each concentration level give you an estimate of precision. More replicates per point = smaller confidence intervals = more reliable predictions.

Critical rule: Never force your calibration line through zero. The y-intercept is actually diagnostic: a line that naturally passes through zero suggests no interferences; a non-zero intercept could indicate endogenous analyte in your blank or an interfering compound.

Students often rely on R² (the correlation coefficient squared) as the sole indicator of calibration quality. This is insufficient and potentially misleading). Since you're deliberately plotting two variables you already know are related (signal vs. concentration), a high R² is expected and doesn't prove the fit is good [1].

Residuals analysis is much more informative. Residuals are the differences between the measured and predicted values. Plotting them reveals patterns:

• Uniform scatter around zero → good fit, no weighting needed
• Cone-shaped pattern (residuals grow with concentration) → variance is proportional to concentration; apply 1/x weighting
• Curved pattern → linear model is inadequate; consider quadratic regression
• Systematic offset → possible missing variable or calculation error

When linear isn't enough: If your residuals form a U-shape instead of scattering randomly, the straight line doesn't fit your data well across the whole range. You could switch to a curved (quadratic) fit, and R² will improve, but that often just papers over the real problem: your detector is saturating at high concentrations. The better fix is to trim your calibration range so it only covers the region where the response is genuinely linear (between the LLOQ and ULOQ). Your protocol allows a quadratic fit "if necessary," but the animation below shows why narrowing the range is usually the sounder approach [1, 6].

Why use 1/x weighting? In mass spectrometry, the scatter (variance) around each data point tends to grow as the concentration goes up. An ordinary (unweighted) regression treats every point equally, so high-concentration points with their large scatter end up dominating the fit. The result: the line fits well at the top of the range but poorly at the bottom, exactly where you most need accuracy (your unknown samples often fall near the low end). A 1/x weighting fixes this by giving lower-concentration points more influence in proportion to their concentration. The animation below lets you compare unweighted and 1/x weighted fits side by side [1].

Every analytical method has boundaries where it becomes unreliable:

Limit of Detection (LOD): The lowest analyte concentration that can be detected, i.e., distinguished from noise with a certain confidence (typically signal-to-noise ratio ≥ 3). You can tell "something is there" but cannot reliably say how much.

Lower Limit of Quantification (LLOQ): The lowest concentration that can be quantified with acceptable accuracy and precision (typically S/N ≥ 10 and ±15–20% accuracy). Below this, your numbers are unreliable.

Upper Limit of Quantification (ULOQ): The highest concentration within the linear (or validated) range. Above this, the detector may saturate, and the relationship between signal and concentration breaks down.

Reporting a concentration below the LLOQ or above the ULOQ without acknowledging it is a major mistake. If your sample concentration falls outside the calibration range, the sample should be diluted (if too high) or concentrated (if too low) and re-measured.

① Error sources

Sample loss is inherent to the method. No extraction is perfect. Even when every step is performed correctly, some analyte is inevitably lost: not all caffeine binds during loading, small amounts wash off with the polar waste, and elution never strips 100% from the sorbent. These are not mistakes, they are limitations of any real sample preparation workflow. The question is not whether you lose analyte, but how much, and whether you can account for it.

On top of these inherent losses, additional errors are introduced by the operator and by the instrument. Understanding the difference matters, because different correction strategies address different categories:

Operator Error

• Pipetting errors: especially small volumes (300 µL sample, 50–700 µL spike)

• SPE sorbent running dry: forgetting to close the valve causes loss of retention and low recovery [2, 3]

• Incomplete elution: not enough solvent or eluting too fast leaves caffeine on the cartridge [2]

• Residual water in eluate: poor chromatography, split peaks, and variable peak areas

• Incorrect dilution factors: forgetting the 1:10 dilution or eluate volume correction

• Contamination: dirty glassware, cross-contamination, or caffeine from skin contact [1]

• Improper standard curve preparation: inaccurate weighing or serial dilution of calibration standards introduces systematic bias into every reported concentration

Analytical Error

• Ion suppression / matrix effects: co-eluting matrix compounds compete in the ion source, suppressing or enhancing the analyte signal [1]

• Calibration outside linear range: extrapolating beyond the LLOQ or ULOQ is unreliable [1, 6]

• Detector response drift: instrument instability causes the same concentration to give different signals [1, 4]

• Poor precision / accuracy: repeated injections of the same sample give variable peak areas (precision); systematic bias from instrument calibration causes values to be consistently high or low (accuracy) [1]

• Integration errors: incorrect baseline or peak boundaries give inaccurate areas [1]

• Interferents / co-elution: ion suppression from co-eluting matrix, or isobaric interferences where a different compound produces ions at the same nominal m/z that the analyser cannot resolve [1, 4]

• Carryover: residual analyte from a previous high-concentration injection contaminates the current sample; common when the autosampler needle or column is not fully washed between runs [1, 4]

• Inadequate calibration model: unweighted regression biases low-concentration predictions; use 1/x weighting [1, 6]

② Recovery: measuring and correcting for error

To measure how much analyte your method loses, you spike a known amount of caffeine into a separate aliquot of the same sample and put it through a fresh SPE cartridge. The spiked sample’s peak area contains both the original caffeine and the spike. By subtracting the unspiked sample’s contribution (corrected for elution volume), the remainder is what you recovered from the spike [1].

Before writing the recovery equation, you need three building blocks:

Concentration from the calibration curve

Your calibration curve relates peak area (A) to concentration. Rearranging y = mx + b gives:

c =
A − b m

This gives cspiked (spiked eluate) and csample (unspiked sample eluate), both in mg/mL.

Volume from gravimetric measurement

Volumetric pipettes are imprecise for small organic-solvent volumes. Instead, weigh the collection tube before and after elution and convert using the density of acetonitrile (ρACN = 0.782 g/mL):

V =
mtube+eluate − mtube ρACN

This gives Vspiked (spiked eluate volume) and Vsample (unspiked sample eluate volume), both in mL.

Expected spike concentration

Unlike cspiked and csample, cspike,exp is not read from the calibration curve. You know exactly how much caffeine standard you spiked in, so the expected concentration in the spiked eluate is:

cspike,exp =
mspike Vspiked

where mspike is the mass of caffeine standard added (mg) and Vspiked is the spiked eluate volume (mL).

Putting it all together:

Recovery (%) =
cspiked · Vspiked − csample · Vsample cspike,exp · Vspiked
× 100

The numerator is the mass of recovered spike (spiked total minus sample background). The denominator is the mass of spike you expected to find if nothing were lost.

VariableDescription
cspikedConcentration of the spiked eluate (mg/mL), from the calibration curve
csampleConcentration of the unspiked sample eluate (mg/mL), from the calibration curve
cspike,expExpected spike concentration = mspike / Vspiked (known, not from the cal curve)
VspikedSpiked eluate volume (mL), measured gravimetrically
VsampleUnspiked sample eluate volume (mL), measured gravimetrically

Acceptable recovery is typically 80 to 120%. Values outside this range indicate systematic loss (low recovery) or matrix enhancement (high recovery).

Because the spike sits in the same matrix as the analyte, ion suppression affects both equally. Standard addition therefore corrects for both SPE losses and matrix effects at once. However, each aliquot is a physically different sample with its own pipetting, extraction, and transfer variability. Standard addition does not correct for operator errors that differ between the spiked and unspiked aliquots.

③ Internal standards: a better correction

An internal standard (IS) is a co-eluting isotope-labelled compound added to every sample before preparation begins. Because the IS travels through the entire workflow alongside the analyte, both experience the same losses. Their ratio stays constant, correcting for recovery / sample loss, ion suppression / matrix effects, detector drift, and all operator errors that occur after the IS is added. It even partially compensates for integration errors, since the IS peak is usually mis-integrated in the same way as the analyte peak. It does not fix isobaric interferences or a wrong calibration model [1].

Q Concept Check 1: Evaluating R²
You run a calibration curve and get R² = 0.998. A colleague tells you "the calibration is perfect." Why should you be skeptical?
R² only measures correlation strength, not fit quality; a high value is expected by design and residuals analysis is needed to reveal problems like nonlinearity
An R² of 0.998 exceeds the theoretical maximum for mass spectrometry data, which suggests the standards were measured multiple times and averaged to artificially reduce variance
R² is only valid for spectrophotometric detectors with linear Beer–Lambert responses; for mass spectrometry data, the Mandel fitting test should be used instead
Any R² below 1.000 indicates the calibration has failed, because a properly prepared standard series with no pipetting errors should produce a perfect linear fit
Correct! This is a commonly misunderstood point. As Gale and Duncan emphasize, using R² as a figure of merit for a calibration curve is statistically inappropriate because the two variables are not independently varying. You intentionally created a relationship between them. A high R² is essentially guaranteed and tells you nothing about whether the model is correct. Residuals analysis reveals whether the variance is homogeneous, whether the relationship is truly linear, and whether weighting is needed. A "perfect" R² can hide serious problems that only become visible in a residuals plot.
Q Concept Check 2: Forcing Through Zero
Why is the calibration line never forced through zero?
Because detector electronic noise always produces a nonzero baseline signal, so a blank measurement can never truly read zero and the origin is not a valid data point
Because most chromatography data systems use ordinary least-squares algorithms that mathematically cannot constrain the intercept to zero without switching to a weighted model
Because the y-intercept is diagnostically useful: it can reveal interferences or endogenous analyte, and forcing through zero distorts the regression
Because removing the intercept term from the linear model causes the slope to become undefined, making it mathematically impossible to solve the regression equation
Correct! The y-intercept of the calibration curve is a diagnostic tool. If the regression line naturally passes through (or near) zero, that's a good sign: it suggests no interferences contribute to the signal at the analyte's retention time. If the intercept is significantly non-zero, it could indicate: (1) endogenous analyte in the blank matrix, (2) a co-eluting interferent contributing to the signal, or (3) contamination. Forcing through zero eliminates this diagnostic and artificially distorts the regression, particularly biasing low-concentration predictions.
